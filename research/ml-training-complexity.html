ML Training Complexity — Time and Compute for Every Common Algorithm

A complete reference of 25 machine learning algorithms with training complexity (Big-O), prediction complexity, memory requirements, scalability ratings, and estimated training time for 1 million samples.

By Michael Lip · Updated April 2026

Methodology

Training complexities are sourced from scikit-learn documentation, original algorithm papers, and standard ML textbooks (Bishop, Murphy, Hastie). Estimated wall-clock times assume 1M samples with 100 float64 features on a modern machine (8-core CPU, 32 GB RAM, single NVIDIA A100 for GPU workloads). Scalability ratings: Excellent (>10M samples practical), Good (1-10M), Fair (100K-1M), Poor (<100K only). Variables: n = samples, d = features, T = trees, I = iterations, K = clusters/classes, E = epochs.

Key Findings

Linear models are absurdly fast. Logistic Regression, Linear SVM, and Naive Bayes all train on 1M samples in under 5 seconds. Their O(n*d) or O(n*d*I) complexity means they scale linearly with data size. They should always be your first baseline.

Kernel SVM is impractical beyond 100K samples. The O(n^2) to O(n^3) training complexity and O(n^2) memory make kernel SVM unusable for large datasets. At 1M samples, the kernel matrix alone requires 8 TB. Use LinearSVC with SGD for large-scale classification.

LightGBM is the fastest tree-based method. With histogram-based splitting (b=256 bins instead of n thresholds) and leaf-wise growth, LightGBM trains on 1M samples in ~10 seconds — 3x faster than XGBoost and 6x faster than scikit-learn's GradientBoosting.

Deep learning compute scales with hardware, not algorithms. A small MLP trains on 1M samples in 5 minutes on a GPU. A 7B-parameter Transformer requires weeks on a GPU cluster. The algorithm complexity is similar (O(n*params*E)), but the constant factor (params) ranges from 10K to 7B — a 700,000x difference.

Frequently Asked Questions

What ML algorithm has the fastest training time?

Naive Bayes at O(n*d) with a single data pass — under 1 second for 1M samples. Linear Regression (Normal Equation) at O(n*d^2+d^3) is also very fast when d is small. SGD-based linear models train in 2-5 seconds on 1M samples.

Why does SVM training scale poorly with dataset size?

Standard SVM (SMO) has O(n^2*d) to O(n^3) complexity because it computes the n x n kernel matrix. For 1M samples in float64, this requires 8 TB of memory. LinearSVC avoids this by solving the primal with SGD in O(n*d), but only supports linear kernels.

How does XGBoost compare to Random Forest in training complexity?

Both are O(T*n*d*log(n)), but XGBoost is sequential (each tree depends on previous) while Random Forest is embarrassingly parallel. With histogram splitting, XGBoost reduces to O(T*n*b) where b=256 bins. For 1M samples: Random Forest ~30s, XGBoost ~20s with defaults.

What is the memory complexity of common ML algorithms?

Linear models: O(d). Trees: O(T*2^depth). KNN: O(n*d) — stores entire dataset. SVM: O(n_sv*d) support vectors. Neural networks: O(params). Kernel SVM: O(n^2) — 8 TB at 1M samples, completely impractical.

How does the number of features affect training time?

Linear models scale linearly: O(n*d). Trees also linear: O(T*n*d*log n). SVM with RBF is O(n^2) regardless of d. PCA is quadratic: O(d^2*n). For very high-dimensional sparse data (d > 100K), use Naive Bayes, Linear SVM, or Logistic Regression with SGD.
